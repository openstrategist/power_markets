The question

Should Texas Cap Large Load Growth?

Grid operators see every unvetted data center connection as a reliability risk externalized onto 30 million Texans. Hyperscalers see every delayed interconnection as ceding AI infrastructure investment to Virginia — permanently. SB 6 forces the argument into the open.

Position A
Grid-First Operators
ERCOT reserve margins are structurally thin. Transmission build lags load by 3–5 years. Large load registration and demand response requirements protect ratepayers and improve planning accuracy.
Position B
Data Center Developers
AI infrastructure is winner-take-most. Texas holds a 40 GW interconnection pipeline advantage. Hyperscalers fund generation build through PPAs. Friction reallocates investment to competitors; it does not eliminate risk.
The central crux

Grid-First / Restrict
ERCOT's reserve margin is structurally thin
ERCOT's planning reserve margin target is 13.75%. In recent summers it has operated below that threshold. Adding 20–30 GW of large load by 2030 without commensurate generation build compounds an already identified risk.
↳ Forced outages · ratepayer liability · PUCT political exposure
Load growth outpaces transmission build by 3–5 years
Transmission permitting and construction in Texas averages 4–6 years. Data center interconnection requests are processed on 18-month timelines. The mismatch creates localized congestion that market signals alone cannot resolve.
↳ Nodal price volatility · stranded generation investment · reliability events
SB 6 registration is disclosure, not a barrier
Requiring loads above 75 MW to register, provide forecasts, and demonstrate demand response capability is a disclosure regime — not a cap. The alternative is anonymized load that distorts ERCOT's planning models and socializes costs invisibly.
↳ Improved ERCOT forecast accuracy · enables cost allocation reform
Data Centers / Compete
AI infrastructure is winner-take-most for states
Virginia, Georgia, and Arizona are actively courting the same hyperscaler capex. Texas's interconnection pipeline — currently ~40 GW, the largest in the US — is a competitive moat that regulatory friction will reallocate to competitors, not eliminate.
↳ Lost tax base · lost jobs · permanent market share shift to Virginia
Large loads are funding the generation Texas needs
Hyperscalers are signing long-term PPAs and funding behind-the-meter generation at scale. Meta's 1.5 GW solar PPA, Amazon's wind portfolio, and Microsoft's nuclear offtake agreements are supply-side investments catalyzed by large load demand — not threats to it.
↳ Net reliability benefit · private capital substituting for ratepayer-funded build
Flexible load is ERCOT's most underused reliability asset
Data centers with UPS systems, battery backup, and variable cooling loads can respond to grid signals in seconds. A properly structured 500 MW data center cluster is a better demand response asset than most peaker plants. The regulatory framework has not caught up.
↳ Demand response replaces peaker investment · lowers system lambda during stress events
